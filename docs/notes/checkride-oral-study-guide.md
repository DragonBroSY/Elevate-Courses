# Private Pilot (ASEL) Checkride Oral — Study Guide
## Focused on Your Missed Knowledge Test Codes

**How the DPE uses these:** Your examiner receives your knowledge test report and is *required* (per the ACS and 14 CFR 61.43) to evaluate the areas you missed. Expect at least one or two questions per code below, usually woven into scenarios. Know these cold and the rest of the oral gets easier.

---

## 1. PA.I.F.K2 — Factors Affecting Performance
*Source: PHAK Ch. 11 (Aircraft Performance)*

**The big four: density altitude, weight, wind, and runway condition.**

**Density altitude** = pressure altitude corrected for non-standard temperature. It's the altitude the airplane *thinks* it's at. High density altitude (high, hot, humid — "the three H's") hurts you three ways:

1. **Engine** produces less power (less dense air = less fuel/air mass per intake stroke)
2. **Propeller** produces less thrust (fewer air molecules to bite)
3. **Wings** produce less lift (need higher true airspeed / longer ground roll to reach flying indicated airspeed)

Standard day: 29.92 "Hg and 15°C at sea level, lapse rate ~2°C per 1,000 ft. Temperature above standard raises density altitude; humidity raises it further (water vapor is less dense than dry air — engines can lose several percent power on very humid days).

**Weight:** More weight = higher stall speed, longer takeoff roll, lower climb rate, lower ceiling, shorter range. Takeoff distance increases *faster* than proportionally with weight (roughly, 10% more weight ≈ 20%+ more takeoff distance).

**Wind:** Headwind shortens takeoff/landing distance and steepens climb gradient over the ground; tailwind does the opposite — dramatically. A tailwind of just 10% of liftoff speed increases takeoff distance ~20%.

**Runway:** Grass, soft, or wet surfaces increase takeoff roll (rolling resistance) and landing roll (reduced braking). Upslope hurts takeoff, helps landing; downslope is the reverse. Contamination (water, ice) risks hydroplaning.

**Practical rule the DPE loves:** POH performance numbers were achieved by test pilots in a new airplane. FAA guidance: add at least a **50% safety margin** to book numbers.

**Likely oral questions:**
- "It's a 95°F July afternoon in Georgia, field elevation 1,000 ft. Walk me through how you'd decide if you can safely depart."
- "How do you calculate density altitude?" (Pressure altitude from setting 29.92 in the Kollsman window or the formula, then correct for temperature via chart/E6B.)
- "Does humidity appear in your POH takeoff chart? How do you account for it?" (Usually no — treat it as an additional conservative margin.)

## 2. PA.I.H.K1f — Carbon Monoxide Poisoning
*Source: PHAK Ch. 17 (Aeromedical Factors), AIM 8-1-4*

**What it is:** Colorless, odorless, tasteless gas from engine exhaust. In most trainers the cabin heat comes from a **shroud (muff) around the exhaust pipe** — a cracked exhaust lets CO into the cabin whenever heat is on. That's why CO risk is highest in **winter with the heater on**.

**Why it's dangerous:** Hemoglobin binds CO roughly **200x more readily than oxygen**, so even small concentrations progressively starve the body of O₂. It compounds hypoxia — effects get worse with altitude. It also takes **hours to days** to purge from the bloodstream; you don't recover just by opening a window.

**Symptoms (progressive):** headache, drowsiness, dizziness, blurred vision, ringing ears, loss of muscle power, tightness across the forehead → unconsciousness. Classic sign sometimes cited: a feeling of warmth/flushing, and you may smell exhaust odor even though CO itself is odorless.

**Immediate actions if suspected:**
1. **Turn cabin heat OFF**
2. Open all fresh air vents / windows if possible
3. Use supplemental oxygen if available
4. **Land as soon as practicable** and seek medical attention
5. Have the exhaust/heater system inspected before further flight

**Prevention:** CO detector in the cockpit (spot cards or electronic), thorough exhaust inspection during preflight and at each inspection.

**Likely oral questions:**
- "It's January, you're using cabin heat, and you develop a dull headache and feel drowsy. What's happening and what do you do?"
- "Why does CO poisoning get worse as you climb?" (Compounds altitude hypoxia — less ambient O₂ partial pressure on top of hemoglobin already blocked by CO.)

## 3. PA.II.A.K3d — Preflight Inspection Using an Appropriate Checklist
*Source: AFH Ch. 2, POH/AFM Section 4, PHAK Ch. 2*

**The "appropriate checklist" is Section 4 of your POH/AFM** — the manufacturer's normal procedures, including the preflight walkaround. The DPE wants to hear that you use the *written* checklist, not memory alone.

**Why checklists matter:** They defend against interruption, distraction, complacency, and fatigue. Acceptable technique: **flow pattern backed up by the checklist** ("do-verify"), or straight "read-do." What's *not* acceptable: pure memory.

**Structure of a good preflight:**
1. **Cabin:** documents on board (ARROW — Airworthiness cert, Registration, Radio license if international, Operating limitations, Weight & balance), required inspections current (**AV1ATE** — Annual, VOR every 30 days if IFR, 100-hour if for hire, Altimeter/pitot-static 24 mo, Transponder 24 mo, ELT 12 mo + battery), master on for fuel gauges/lights check, master off
2. **Walkaround per POH sequence:** control surfaces free and secure, hinges/bolts, fuel sump samples (check color, water, sediment), fuel quantity visually verified, oil level, propeller/spinner nicks, pitot tube and static ports clear, tires/brakes/struts, antennas, tiedowns/chocks/pitot cover removed
3. **Discrepancies:** anything questionable → is the airplane still airworthy? (91.7 — pilot in command determines the aircraft is in condition for safe flight; inoperative equipment handled per 91.213)

**Likely oral questions:**
- "During preflight you find a fuel sample that looks cloudy — what do you do?" (Keep sampling until clear; water/sediment means investigate further, don't fly until resolved.)
- "Why not just preflight from memory? You've done it a hundred times."
- "What documents must be aboard, and which inspections must be current for today's flight?"

## 4. PA.III.A.K2 — Radio Communication Procedures & ATC Phraseology
*Source: AIM Ch. 4 Section 2, PHAK Ch. 14*

**The universal format — who, who, where, what:**
> "Peachtree Tower, Skyhawk Three-Four-Five-Six-Tango, ten miles south with information Bravo, inbound full stop."

**Key phraseology (AIM 4-2):**
- **Roger** = I received your transmission (NOT yes)
- **Affirm** = yes; **Negative** = no
- **Wilco** = received and will comply
- **Say again** = repeat; **Stand by** = wait
- Numbers: altitudes as "four thousand five hundred"; headings digit-by-digit "heading two-seven-zero"; frequencies "one-two-two-point-eight"
- Full phonetic alphabet (Alpha through Zulu) — be ready to recite

**Mandatory readbacks:** runway **hold short** instructions, runway assignment, "line up and wait," any clearance onto/across a runway, LAHSO acceptance. Always read back with your callsign.

**Towered field:** get ATIS first, contact ground/tower with the format above, comply or say "unable." Clearance to "taxi to Runway 27" authorizes crossing intervening runways *only if explicitly cleared to cross each one* (post-2010 rule: explicit crossing clearance required for every runway).

**Non-towered field (AIM 4-1-9):** self-announce on CTAF — position, intentions, airport name at beginning *and* end ("Winder traffic, Skyhawk 3456T, entering left downwind Runway 31, Winder"). Monitor from 10 miles out. **Never say "any traffic in the area please advise"** — the AIM specifically calls this out as improper.

**Lost comms VFR:** squawk **7600**, look for light gun signals at towered fields (steady green = cleared to land, flashing green = return for landing, steady red = give way/continue circling, flashing red = airport unsafe do not land, flashing white = return to starting point, alternating red/green = extreme caution).

**Likely oral questions:**
- "You're inbound to a Class D. Walk me through your radio calls from 15 miles out."
- "Tower says 'hold short Runway 27.' What do you say?"
- "Radio fails in the pattern at a towered field — what do you do and what do the light signals mean?"

## 5. PA.IV.A.R2d & PA.IV.B.R2d — Wake Turbulence (Takeoff and Landing)
*Source: AIM 7-4, PHAK Ch. 5*

This showed up twice on your report, so expect the DPE to spend real time here.

**The physics:** Wingtip vortices form whenever a wing produces lift — greatest when the generating aircraft is **HEAVY, CLEAN, and SLOW** (i.e., rotation/takeoff and approach/landing). Vortices sink at ~**400–500 fpm**, level off about 500–900 ft below the aircraft's path, and **drift with the wind**. A **light quartering tailwind** is the worst case — it can hold the upwind vortex over the runway and push it into your touchdown zone.

**Avoidance — the rule is always the same idea: stay above their path, and touch down/lift off beyond where their wake was created.**

*Departing behind a large aircraft that departed:* Note their **rotation point**. Lift off **before** that point, then climb **above their climb path**, turning **upwind** if able.

*Departing behind a large aircraft that just landed:* Lift off **beyond** their touchdown point.

*Landing behind a large aircraft that's landing:* Stay **at or above** their final approach path, land **beyond** their touchdown point.

*Landing behind a large aircraft that's departing:* Land **well before** their rotation point.

*Intersecting runways:* cross above their flight path, or plan touchdown before the intersection if they rotated beyond it.

**Time buffers:** ATC applies wake separation (e.g., 2–3 minutes behind heavies/departing large aircraft), but when you're VFR and accept a clearance ("cleared for immediate takeoff"), **wake avoidance is your responsibility** — you may decline and wait. Helicopters generate downwash vortices too; avoid operating within ~3 rotor diameters of a hovering helicopter.

**Likely oral questions:**
- "A Gulfstream just rotated halfway down the runway and tower clears you for immediate takeoff. What do you do?" (Best answer: evaluate — if you can't lift off before its rotation point and out-climb its path upwind, tell tower you'll wait for wake turbulence separation.)
- "Winds are calm-to-light quartering tailwind and you're landing behind a 737. Why is that the most dangerous wind condition?"

## 6. PA.IV.D.K1 — Stabilized Approach & Energy Management
*Source: AFH Ch. 9, FAA-H-8083-3 energy management chapter*

**Stabilized approach — the elements (be able to list them):**
- **Constant-angle glidepath** toward a fixed aiming point (the point doesn't move in the windscreen)
- **On speed:** ~**1.3 × Vso** on final (adjusted for gusts — add half the gust factor)
- **Configured:** correct flap setting, gear (if retractable), trim set — no big changes close-in
- **Aligned:** on extended centerline, drift corrected
- **Constant, appropriate descent rate** with only small power/pitch corrections needed

**Personal go-around gate:** if the approach isn't stabilized by a defined altitude (many use 300–500 ft AGL for VFR), **go around**. Say this proactively — DPEs love hearing a hard gate.

**Energy management concept:** Your total energy = **altitude (potential) + airspeed (kinetic)**, managed with **power (throttle)** and **drag (flaps/slip)**, exchanged through **pitch**. High and fast = too much total energy → reduce power, add drag, S-turn/slip, or go around. Low and slow = energy deficit → add power *now* (pitch alone just trades one deficit for another). The classic teaching model: **pitch for airspeed, power for glidepath** on approach — but really they work together as one energy problem.

**Why it matters:** unstabilized approaches are the leading contributor to loss-of-control and runway excursion accidents. Landing long, floating, porpoising, and ballooning almost always start with excess energy on final.

**Likely oral questions:**
- "Define a stabilized approach. What are your personal criteria for going around?"
- "You're on final, high and fast. What are your options, in order?" (Reduce power, full flaps, forward slip, and if still not stabilized by your gate — go around.)
- "Why is being 10 knots fast on final a big deal in a 172?" (Kinetic energy goes with velocity *squared* — float, longer landing roll, increased LOC risk.)

## 7. PA.IV.F.K3 — Wind Correction on Approach and Landing
*Source: AFH Ch. 9 (Approaches and Landings — crosswind section)*

**Two crosswind techniques:**

1. **Crab method:** Wings level, nose pointed into the wind so ground track stays on centerline. Comfortable, coordinated — but you **must transition** (kick out the crab) during roundout, or you'll touch down side-loading the gear.

2. **Wing-low (sideslip) method:** Lower the **upwind wing** with aileron to stop drift; use **opposite rudder** to keep the nose aligned with the runway. Cross-controlled by design. Touch down on the **upwind main wheel first**, then downwind main, then nosewheel.

**Most common technique:** crab down final for comfort, transition to wing-low in the roundout/flare.

**After touchdown:** progressively increase **aileron into the wind** as you slow (full deflection by taxi speed), rudder for directional control. Know your **taxi wind-correction diagram** (climb into a quartering headwind, dive away from a quartering tailwind).

**Gusts:** add **half the gust factor** to approach speed (e.g., winds 12G20 → add 4 kts). Consider partial flaps in strong/gusty crosswinds per your POH.

**Limits:** know your airplane's **maximum demonstrated crosswind component** (e.g., 15 kts for a 172S) — it's not a legal limitation, but it *is* what a test pilot demonstrated; your personal minimum should be at or below it. Be able to compute crosswind component (rule of thumb: wind angle 30° ≈ half the wind speed is crosswind; 45° ≈ 70%; 60°+ ≈ all of it).

**Likely oral questions:**
- "Explain the difference between the crab and wing-low methods. When do you transition?"
- "Winds are 240 at 15, landing Runway 31. What's your crosswind component, and is it within your limits?" (70° off ≈ nearly all 15 kts crosswind — likely a no-go or different runway.)
- "What does 'maximum demonstrated crosswind' mean legally?"

## 8. PA.IX.D.K1 — Immediate Action Items & Emergency Checklists
*Source: AFH Ch. 18 (Emergency Procedures), POH Section 3*

**The hierarchy: AVIATE → NAVIGATE → COMMUNICATE.** Fly the airplane first, always.

**Immediate action items (memory items / "boldface"):** the first critical steps of an emergency that must be executed **from memory** because there's no time to read — e.g., engine failure after takeoff: pitch for best glide, land mostly straight ahead. Once the airplane is under control and time permits, **back up memory items with the written checklist** (POH Section 3) to catch anything missed and complete the non-memory steps.

**Engine failure flow (the classic ABCDE):**
- **A** — Airspeed: pitch for **best glide (Vg)** immediately, trim for it
- **B** — Best field: pick a landing spot, turn toward it, plan the pattern
- **C** — Checklist: attempt restart flow from memory (fuel selector both/fullest, mixture rich, carb heat on, mags check, primer locked), then confirm with the written checklist
- **D** — Declare: **121.5** / current freq, squawk **7700**, MAYDAY ×3
- **E** — Exit/secure: if landing off-field — fuel off, mixture cut, mags off, master off (after flaps set), doors unlatched, seatbelts tight

**Key distinctions the DPE probes:**
- Which items in *your* airplane's POH are memory items vs. reference items? (Know your Section 3 cold — engine fire in flight, engine failure, electrical fire, door open, etc.)
- **Engine failure right after takeoff:** the "impossible turn" — below your personal turn-back altitude, land within ~30° of straight ahead. Don't stall trying to make it back.
- Engine **fire** in flight: fuel/mixture off, and typically **do not restart** — emergency descent and land.

**Likely oral questions:**
- "Engine quits at 3,000 AGL over this sectional — talk me through everything, in order."
- "Why do we memorize some checklist items and read others?"
- "Engine failure at 300 ft after takeoff — what do you do and what do you NOT do?"

## 9. PA.V.B.K4 — Rectangular Course ↔ Traffic Pattern
*Source: AFH Ch. 6 (Ground Reference Maneuvers)*

**The whole point of the rectangular course is that it's a traffic pattern rehearsal at ground-reference altitude (600–1,000 AGL).** Be ready to draw the parallel explicitly:

| Rectangular course | Traffic pattern |
|---|---|
| Four legs around a field | Upwind, crosswind, downwind, base, final |
| Maintain uniform distance from the boundary | Maintain proper pattern distance from the runway |
| Crab on each leg to hold ground track | Crab on crosswind/downwind/base to hold pattern shape |
| Vary bank in turns based on groundspeed | Same — e.g., downwind-to-base with a tailwind-becoming-crosswind requires a steeper initial bank; base-to-final into a headwind needs shallower bank |
| Divide attention: outside references + coordination + altitude | Divide attention: traffic, radio, checklists, and flying the pattern |

**Key wind logic:** turns from a **tailwind leg** (high groundspeed) require **steeper bank** and an earlier/faster roll-in; turns into a **headwind leg** (low groundspeed) require **shallower bank**. This is exactly why the downwind-to-base turn (typically highest groundspeed) tempts pilots into over-banking or skidding the base-to-final turn — the classic stall/spin scenario.

**Likely oral question:**
- "Why do we teach the rectangular course? How does wind affect each turn in the traffic pattern?"
- "Why is the base-to-final turn the most dangerous in the pattern?" (Overshooting centerline with a tailwind on base → temptation to skid the turn with rudder → cross-controlled stall at low altitude.)

## 10. PA.VI.A.K5b — Wind Correction Angle
*Source: PHAK Ch. 16 (Navigation)*

**Definitions — keep them straight:**
- **Course:** intended path over the ground (true course from the sectional; magnetic course after variation)
- **Heading:** where the nose points
- **Track:** actual path over the ground
- **Wind correction angle (WCA):** the difference between course and heading needed so track = course. You **crab into the wind**.

**The chain (memorize):**
**True Course ± WCA = True Heading; TH ± Variation = Magnetic Heading; MH ± Deviation = Compass Heading.**
Mnemonic: **TVMDC** — "True Virgins Make Dull Company," and **East is least, West is best** for variation.

**Finding WCA:** the E6B wind side (you know this — wind dot method: set wind direction under the true index, mark wind speed up, rotate to true course, read WCA left/right of centerline and groundspeed under the grommet). Crosswind pushes you off track → point the nose into it.

**Rules of thumb for the oral:**
- Full crosswind component (kts) ÷ (TAS in miles per minute × 60)... simpler version: **WCA ≈ crosswind component ÷ TAS × 60**. E.g., 20 kt direct crosswind at 120 KTAS ≈ 10° WCA.
- Headwind/tailwind component changes **groundspeed**, crosswind component changes **WCA** — pure headwind needs zero crab.

**Likely oral questions:**
- "On your cross-country plan, how did you get from true course to compass heading?" (Walk the TVMDC chain with your actual numbers.)
- "In flight, how do you know your computed WCA is working, and what do you do if you're drifting?" (Compare track to course using checkpoints/GPS; adjust crab, re-establish, note the new heading.)

## Final Prep Tips

**Bring and know your numbers:** V-speeds, max demonstrated crosswind, best glide, and your personal minimums. For every topic above, tie your answer back to *your specific airplane's POH*.

**Answer format that works with DPEs:** direct answer first → cite the source (POH / PHAK / AIM / FAR) → apply it to the scenario. Don't volunteer beyond the question.

**Scenario thread to expect:** since your misses cluster around performance, approaches/landings, wind, and emergencies, a likely scenario is: *"Hot summer day, gusty crosswind, short runway — plan the flight, fly the approach, and the engine quits in the pattern."* If you can talk through that one scenario end-to-end using everything above, you've covered every missed code.

Good luck — you've got this. 🛩️
